title: "Time To Be Productive On Weekends"
date: "2025-06-03"
is_published: true
---

I feel guilty whenever I do nothing on my day off from work. As someone who works 9 to 5, I can barely afford more productive time on weekdays. After my 9 to 5 job, I do my chores and work out, which usually finishes at 8:00 PM. Then I have time to work, which would be less than 4 hours since I need to sleep by midnight at the latest.

I once talked to my friend about my concern and how I should allocate my day off. They just said, "That's why it's called a day off—you just do nothing." Sure, you need to take time to just relax. But I still feel stressed about wasting my time. I have "nothing to be proud of", and that's why I feel I have to "work" or be productive. The work that should be done on weekends doesn't have to be literal "working." It can be in the form of traveling, learning, trying out new activities, working out—heck, even exploring new places counts as productive for me.

The problem for me is that I usually do most of them spontaneously and not efficiently enough, especially on long weekends. I guess I need to plan my weekends more meticulously to avoid "do nothing" day.

> Only a slave quantifies its existence through productivity

Such a beautiful phrases. It hit differently when I re-read what I wrote. Maybe "productive" was a poor choice of words - it sounds like I'm quantizing my life as a metrics. What I actually meant was closer to not wanting to feel ignorant. There's so much I don't know — about the world, about society, about things that matter to me. Traveling, exploring, trying new things — for me, that's less about "output" and more about not wanting to feel like I'm standing still while the world moves.

I also want to address "nothing to be proud of" — that was the wrong way to think, and I realize that now. That kind of mindset only breeds resentment toward yourself. There's nothing productive about measuring your worth against what you haven't done yet. Even if nothing feels measurable, I'm still proud of who I am and what I've managed to achieve — and honestly, just showing up every day and living through it is already something worth acknowledging.

So I think what Jett Franzen said is completely valid. I don't want to chase numbers — I want to chase knowledge and experience, and learn where my limits are. To know when to stop, to know when something is enough. Not out of greed, not out of ego.
